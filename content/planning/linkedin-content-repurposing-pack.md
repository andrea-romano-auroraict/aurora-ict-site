## LinkedIn Post Drafts (10)

### 1) Educational: What a Technology Health Check actually gives leadership
**Angle:** Educational / practical clarity

One of the biggest misconceptions in mid-growth businesses is that a technology review is "just an IT audit."  
A practical Technology Health Check should be much more useful than that.

It gives leadership a clear picture of:
- where spend is leaking
- where risk is accumulating
- where systems and vendors are creating avoidable friction
- what to do in the next 90 days (in sequence)

This is less about technical detail and more about decision quality.

If decisions are currently happening one ticket and one renewal at a time, this kind of baseline quickly improves confidence at leadership level.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If you'd like, I can share a simple 90-day prioritisation structure we use after a Technology Health Check."

---

### 2) Problem/insight-led: Rising IT spend without clear outcomes
**Angle:** Problem insight

A pattern I see often: technology spend increases each year, but leadership still can't clearly explain what improved.

Usually this isn't because teams aren't working hard. It's because decisions are distributed across projects, vendors, and urgent requests—with no shared prioritisation model.

The result:
- duplicate tools
- rushed renewals
- unclear ownership
- weak return visibility

You don't fix that with another platform. You fix it with better sequencing, governance, and accountability.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If this feels familiar, start by mapping spend, ownership, and business outcomes on one page—clarity appears quickly."

---

### 3) Common business mistakes: Tool overlap and vendor sprawl
**Angle:** Mistake-led

Common growth-stage mistake: solving each new issue with a new tool.

In isolation, each choice seems sensible. Over time, the stack becomes expensive, inconsistent, and hard to manage.

What this quietly creates:
- integration drag
- data inconsistency
- support overhead
- weaker negotiating leverage with vendors

The goal isn't "fewer tools" at all costs. The goal is a cleaner, more intentional stack aligned to real workflows.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"A practical first step is a vendor and SaaS review focused on overlap, contracts, and operational fit."

---

### 4) Practical advice: 5 signs you've outgrown ad hoc IT decisions
**Angle:** Checklist / diagnostic

Not sure whether your business has outgrown ad hoc IT decisions? Pressure-test these signs:

1) Spend is rising but accountability is blurred  
2) Teams rely on recurring manual workarounds  
3) Security controls haven't kept pace with growth  
4) Priorities are driven by urgency, not sequence  
5) Critical system knowledge sits with one person/provider

If 2–3 of these are true, you're usually dealing with an operating model issue—not a one-off technical problem.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If useful, I can turn this into a short internal scorecard your leadership team can review in 20 minutes."

---

### 5) Educational: Moving from reactive IT to a roadmap without bureaucracy
**Angle:** Educational / strategic

Reactive IT is normal early on. But once complexity grows, it creates rework and slows execution.

A practical roadmap is not a 60-page strategy deck.  
It's a decision framework that clarifies:
- current constraints
- priority initiatives linked to business goals
- realistic sequencing (12/36/60 months)
- ownership and governance checkpoints

The right roadmap reduces noise and improves delivery confidence.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If your team is stuck in urgent mode, start with a roadmap workshop focused on sequencing—not theory."

---

### 6) Practical advice: Security baseline for growing businesses
**Angle:** Practical / risk reduction

Security maturity doesn't start with buying every new tool.

For growing businesses, a practical baseline usually starts with consistency in a few areas:
- identity and access controls
- device and endpoint hygiene
- tested backup and recovery
- patching cadence
- phishing resilience
- basic incident readiness

This is about resilience that teams can actually maintain under business pressure.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If your controls feel fragmented, pick one baseline area per month and build from there."

---

### 7) Common business mistakes: Overcomplicating security
**Angle:** Mistake-led / myth-busting

A costly security mistake I keep seeing: complexity before fundamentals.

Examples:
- too many tools before core controls are stable
- feature-led buying with unclear ownership
- one-off security projects with no ongoing rhythm

Strong posture usually comes from calm basics done consistently.

Security should reduce business risk without creating operational drag.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"Before adding another tool, ask: which business risk does this reduce, and who will own it monthly?"

---

### 8) Offer-led (light): How Aurora ICT's modular model works
**Angle:** Light offer / positioning

One reason advisory engagements fail: they try to do everything at once.

Our model is intentionally modular:
1) **Assess** current cost, risk, systems, vendors, and friction  
2) **Prioritise** quick wins and staged decisions  
3) **Support** only where targeted help improves outcomes  
4) **Simplify** so the business is more self-sufficient

Start where needed. Continue where value is clear. Stop when capability is strong.

This keeps advisory support proportionate, practical, and business-first.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"Happy to share examples of how businesses sequence this model over their first 90 days."

---

### 9) Problem/insight-led: Key-person dependency is a strategic risk
**Angle:** Risk insight

If one person holds most of the context on your systems, vendors, and architecture decisions, that's not just an operational issue—it's a strategic risk.

When knowledge is concentrated:
- response time slows when people are unavailable
- vendor decisions become harder to challenge
- roadmap decisions get delayed
- continuity risk increases

A practical maturity step is documenting core decisions, dependencies, and ownership before the next growth push.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"Leadership teams should treat knowledge concentration as a board-level resilience topic, not just an IT concern."

---

### 10) Practical + offer-led: A calm first step when everything feels messy
**Angle:** Practical first step / soft offer

When technology feels messy, many leaders assume the answer is a major transformation.

Often, the better first move is much lighter:
- establish a baseline of systems, spend, risks, and dependencies
- identify quick wins that reduce drag immediately
- set a focused 90-day priority sequence
- decide what *not* to do right now

Clarity first. Then targeted action.

That approach usually delivers better outcomes than rushing into large-scale change.

**Suggested CTA / close:**  
"If you're deciding where to start, a focused Technology Health Check is designed exactly for this stage."
